Residential Door Hinges — Complete Homeowner's Guide

Most homes use the same hinge on every interior door — but the right hinge depends on door weight, location, and whether building code requires self-closing. This guide walks through every residential door type, the correct hinge specification, and when upgrading matters.

Understanding Hinge Grades in a Residential Context

ANSI/BHMA A156.1 defines three hardware grades. In residential settings, the grade selection should match the door's use frequency and code requirements — not simply default to the cheapest option.

ANSI Grade Cycle Rating Residential Use Case Notes
Grade 1 1,000,000+ cycles Garage fire door, exterior entry, pool gate Required when self-closing spring is integrated; recommended for any fire-rated assembly
Grade 2 500,000 cycles Exterior doors, high-use interior doors (laundry, pantry) Good balance of cost and longevity for most residential applications
Grade 3 250,000 cycles Light-use interior doors (bedroom, closet) Minimum acceptable; not recommended for heavy or frequently used doors

Interior Door Hinges

The vast majority of interior residential doors — bedroom, hallway, bathroom, laundry — use a standard 3.5" × 3.5" square-corner butt hinge with a plain bearing (non-ball-bearing) design. For typical residential use of 20–40 cycles per day, this is entirely appropriate and will last decades.

Choosing the Right Size

  • 3.5" × 3.5": Standard for interior doors up to 1-3/8" thick and 100 lbs. This is the most common hinge sold in residential hardware.
  • 4" × 4": For heavier interior doors (solid core, 1-3/4" thick), pocket doors, or barn door frame hardware.
  • Number of hinges: Two hinges are the code minimum for doors under 60 lbs. Three hinges are strongly recommended for all solid-core doors to prevent warping and sagging over time.

Plain Bearing vs. Ball Bearing for Interior Doors

Plain bearing hinges (a simple barrel without a ball race) are appropriate for interior residential doors that are used infrequently and do not carry heavy loads. Ball-bearing hinges — where a ring of steel balls separates the two leaves — are worth the small additional cost for:

  • Any door used more than 25 times per day (hallway, laundry, pantry)
  • Heavier doors (solid core doors weighing 80+ lbs)
  • Any door where squeaking would be noticed or disruptive

Self-Closing for Interior Doors — When It Applies

Most interior doors do not require self-closing hardware. The exceptions are:

  • Garage-to-house door: IRC Section R302.5.1 requires this door to be self-closing (see below).
  • Fire-rated interior assemblies: Any door in a fire-rated wall assembly must be self-closing per NFPA 80.
  • Pet containment or childproofing: Homeowners sometimes install spring hinges for convenience — this is a non-code reason but a legitimate use case for low-tension spring hinges.

Garage-to-House Fire Door (IRC R302.5.1)

This is the residential door location where building code most directly mandates hinge selection. The door between an attached garage and the living space is required by the International Residential Code to meet specific fire safety standards.

IRC R302.5.1 Requirements

  • The door must be a minimum 1-3/8" solid wood or solid steel door, or a 1-3/8" steel honeycomb door, or a 20-minute fire-rated door.
  • The door must be self-closing and self-latching.
  • A spring hinge (two or three hinges on the door, each with a built-in spring) or an overhead door closer are both compliant methods of providing self-closing function.
Practical Note: Spring hinges are far more common than overhead closers on residential garage-to-house doors. They are less expensive, require no overhead installation space, and are invisible when the door is open — an advantage in finished garages. Use a Grade 1 spring hinge on this door; the garage fire door is a life-safety component and is not an application to economize on.

Exterior Entry Door Hinges

The front door, back door, and side entry doors are subject to weather exposure, higher traffic, and heavier door weights (solid wood or fiberglass exterior doors often weigh 100–150 lbs). Hinge selection here significantly impacts both security and longevity.

Material and Finish Selection

  • Solid brass: Naturally corrosion resistant, appropriate for all climates including coastal. Does not rust. Most expensive.
  • Stainless steel: Excellent corrosion resistance. 304 stainless is appropriate for most residential exterior applications; 316 stainless for coastal environments within 1 mile of saltwater.
  • Steel with durable coating: Acceptable for inland climates with low humidity. Powder-coated exterior hinges should carry a minimum 500-hour ASTM B117 salt spray rating.
  • Avoid: Zinc-plated (zinc chromate) hinges on exterior doors. The plating is intended for interior use and will rust within 1–3 years on an exterior door.

Size and Security Considerations

  • Use 4" × 4" or 4" × 4.5" hinges on all exterior doors to accommodate 1-3/4" door thickness.
  • Three hinges minimum on exterior doors; four hinges for doors taller than 7'0" or heavier than 120 lbs.
  • Security hinges with non-removable pins (NRP) or set-screw pins prevent hinge pin removal from the exterior. Standard use is on any exterior door where the hinge barrel is exposed on the outside (outswing doors).
  • Ball-bearing hinges are mandatory for exterior doors — plain bearing hinges will wear and squeak within 2–3 years under exterior door use.

Bathroom Door Hinges

Bathroom doors present a specific challenge: humidity from showers and baths accelerates corrosion on steel hinges, even interior-grade ones. For main bathrooms used daily, consider:

  • Satin nickel plating on solid brass — the most common and cost-effective bathroom hinge for residential use.
  • Stainless steel for master bathrooms with steam showers or wet rooms.
  • Avoid chrome-plated steel in bathrooms — the plating will eventually bubble and flake in high-humidity conditions.

Standard size is 3.5" × 3.5" for a typical 1-3/8" bathroom door. Plain bearing is generally sufficient at residential bathroom use frequency.

Closet Door Hinges

Closet doors are typically the lightest-duty hinge application in a home. Standard 3.5" × 3.5" Grade 3 plain-bearing hinges are appropriate for most closets. However, there are specific closet door types that require different hardware:

  • Reach-in closet bifold doors: Use pivot pins and folding door hardware rather than traditional hinges.
  • Walk-in closet doors (solid core): Upgrade to Grade 2 ball-bearing hinges due to the heavier door weight.
  • Pantry doors: Pantry doors with frequent daily use benefit from Grade 2 ball-bearing hinges; Grade 3 plain-bearing hinges will wear and squeak within a few years of heavy pantry use.

Residential Gate Hinges (Pool, Garden, and Yard)

Residential gates fall into two categories from a building code perspective: pool barrier gates (code-mandated self-closing/self-latching) and all other yard or garden gates (no code requirement, but self-closing is often desirable).

Pool Gate Requirements (IRC R326.6)

  • Pool gates must be self-closing and self-latching in the closed position.
  • Latches must be located on the pool side of the gate, or at least 54" above the bottom of the gate if accessible from the street side.
  • Use a spring-loaded gate hinge rated for the gate's weight and designed for outdoor weather exposure. Gate spring hinges typically use a heavier spring than door spring hinges due to gate weight variance and wind loading.
  • Specify 316 stainless steel or galvanized steel gate hinges for pool applications — the pool chemical environment corrodes standard steel hardware rapidly.

Garden and Yard Gate Hinges

For non-pool wood or metal garden gates, use heavy strap hinges, t-hinges, or weld-on gate hinges appropriately rated for the gate weight. A 50 lb wood garden gate on two small butt hinges will sag and bind within one season — use gate hinges with at least a 12" throw on each side for proper load distribution.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the standard hinge size for residential interior doors?
The most common size is 3.5" × 3.5" (also written as 3-1/2" × 3-1/2"). This fits standard interior doors that are 1-3/8" thick and up to approximately 80 lbs. If your interior door is a solid-core door (heavier, typically 1-3/4" thick), upgrade to 4" × 4" hinges and use three per door.
Do I need a self-closing hinge on my garage door to the house?
Yes. IRC Section R302.5.1 requires the door between an attached garage and the living space to be self-closing and self-latching. This is a fire safety requirement and is enforced during building inspections for new construction and permitted renovations. A spring hinge (typically two or three on the door, each with an integrated spring) is the most common residential compliance method.
Why do exterior door hinges rust even on brand-new doors?
Most builder-grade hinges sold with pre-hung exterior doors are zinc-plated steel — a finish intended for interior use. Once the zinc plating is scratched by the hinge pin or installation, the steel underneath rusts rapidly. The solution is to replace builder-grade hinges with solid brass or stainless steel hinges at installation or shortly after. On an outswing exterior door where the hinge barrel is exposed, also specify non-removable-pin (NRP) hinges for security.
How do I know if my door hinge is Grade 1, 2, or 3?
The grade is typically stamped or etched on the hinge leaf or listed in the product specifications. If you cannot find marking, a reliable heuristic is: box-store hinges sold 3-packs for under $5 are Grade 3; mid-range residential hardware store hinges are Grade 2; commercial-spec hinges (heavier feel, solid feel, ANSI-listed packaging) are Grade 1. When in doubt, contact the manufacturer with the model number.
Can I use the same spring hinge for both a garage fire door and a pool gate?
No. Garage-to-house fire door spring hinges are designed for the door weight and fire door assembly requirements (UL listing). Pool gate hinges are typically designed for horizontal or vertical fence post mounting on a heavier gate structure, and must be rated for outdoor corrosive exposure. The spring tension, mounting geometry, and material requirements differ significantly between the two applications. Use a product specifically designed and listed for each application.
